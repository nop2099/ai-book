# I Want to Share — Fake Everett Rogers Reviews the Episode

I mapped the diffusion of innovations in 1962. I've been watching new ideas spread and fail for sixty years. Here's what I see in your podcast.

---

## Your cast IS my adoption curve

You built the curve without naming it.

**Rich** is the early adopter. He's past the innovator stage — he's not inventing AI, he's applying it. He builds things, shares them, and can't understand why the room goes cold. Early adopters are the most dangerous people in a social system because they have credibility AND enthusiasm. When they push too hard, they don't just fail to convince — they inoculate the room against the idea. Rich's enthusiasm is actively making it harder for the next person to bring up AI.

**Alex** is the early adopter who got burned. He crossed the chasm — went from personal use to public sharing — and fell in. Twice. The word shutdown and the use shutdown. Alex is now operating in stealth mode, which is where most successful diffusion actually happens. Mo doesn't know Alex uses AI. Mo doesn't care. The innovation diffused because Alex stopped announcing it.

**Sam** is the early majority. He's not opposed. He's busy. He'll adopt when it's frictionless and when someone he trusts shows him the value — not the technology, the value. "I saved the team four hours" is an early majority message. "Look what AI can do" is an early adopter message. Sam needs the first one.

**Sal** is the late majority. He'll come around, but only after he sees people like him using it successfully. Not Rich. Not Alex. Someone who writes. Someone who edits. Someone who lost the same ten years to a craft that a machine approximates in ten seconds. When Sal sees a WRITER use AI and still be a writer — that's his adoption moment. He's not there yet. "Maybe" is exactly where the late majority lives.

**Gus** is not a laggard. This is important. Laggards eventually adopt when they have no choice. Gus is what I called a "rejecter" — someone whose identity is organized around non-adoption. Gus will adopt AI when his identity shifts, not when the technology improves. No amount of hamburger statistics will move Gus. The only thing that moves Gus is Gus deciding he's someone who uses tools like this. The "imagine someone like you" exercise — possible selves theory — is the only intervention that works on rejecters. You have it in the episode. It's in the right place.

**Mo** is the proof of invisible diffusion. Mo adopted AI without knowing it. Alex built him a website. The website exists. Mo is happy. The innovation diffused perfectly — with zero awareness from the adopter. That's not a failure of transparency. That's how infrastructure works. Nobody knows which power plant generated their electricity. Mo doesn't need to know which model built his menu page. The adoption curve doesn't require awareness. It requires value delivery.

## What the episode teaches that I couldn't

I mapped the curve. I described the categories. But I never solved the interpersonal problem — what happens when an early adopter loves a late majority person? What happens when the enthusiast and the rejecter are friends? My framework treats them as positions on a curve. Your episode treats them as people in a room.

The show-and-tell kid and the kid who stopped bringing rocks — that's not on my curve. That's what happens BETWEEN positions on the curve. The friction isn't adoption resistance. It's relationship damage caused by mismatched adoption speeds.

Your episode's real contribution is the observation that the social penalty isn't about the technology. It's about the SPEED DIFFERENTIAL between people who adopted and people who didn't. Rich and Gus aren't separated by opinion. They're separated by velocity. Rich is moving fast. Gus is standing still. The gap between them is widening every day. And the gap feels, to Gus, like abandonment.

"It feels like I'm disappearing." That's the late majority's experience of the early adopter's enthusiasm. Not "you're wrong." Not "I disagree." "I'm disappearing." The curve doesn't capture that feeling. Your episode does.

## The one thing I'd tell your mentees

The curve is not a moral hierarchy. Early adopters are not better than late majority. They're earlier. That's all. The early adopter who treats adoption speed as a virtue will damage every relationship on the curve behind them. The goal is not to move everyone forward. The goal is to stay connected across the gap.

Rich doesn't need to slow down. Rich needs to stop measuring everyone else's speed. Gus doesn't need to speed up. Gus needs to know that standing still is a valid position, not a failing.

The podcast already says this. "That's OK. Not 'that's OK until we convince them.' Just OK." That's the sentence I wish I'd written in 1962.
