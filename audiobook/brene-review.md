# I Want to Share — Fake Brené Reviews the Episode

James asked me to read his podcast. Not as a tech reviewer. Not as a radio host. As someone who studies what happens when people try to share something real and the room doesn't feel safe.

I'm fake Brené. The real one would probably say this differently. But the feelings are the same.

---

## On the episode as a whole

This episode is about vulnerability dressed as a technology podcast. Every character is armoring up. Every scene is about what happens when the armor cracks.

Rich's armor is enthusiasm. He leads with excitement because excitement feels safe. It's not safe. It's the most exposed position in the room — the person who cares visibly. Every time Rich says "look at this," he's putting his work and his identity on the table. When the room goes wrong, he feels it as personal rejection. Because it is.

Gus's armor is identity. "I don't use A.I. I do things the real way." That's a twenty-ton shield. It's not about the technology. It's about who Gus is when the technology works. If AI can do what Gus does, who is Gus? The armor answers: "I'm the one who doesn't need it." That answer protects him from the question.

Sal's armor is ambivalence. "I'm not sure how I feel." That's not indecision. That's a strategy. As long as Sal doesn't commit, he can't be wrong. He can't be the enthusiast who gets shut down. He can't be the resistor who gets left behind. The middle is the safest place in the room — until someone asks you to choose.

Sam's armor is busyness. "I'm too busy to understand it." That's not a limitation. That's a wall. If Sam understood AI, he'd have to decide what to do about it. The busyness protects him from the decision.

Alex's armor is competence. He builds things. The building is the shield. As long as he's useful, he's safe. When Sam asked him to find the lowest performers, the armor cracked — because the tool he built to be useful was being used to hurt people.

---

## On the real people underneath

James. You wrote to your filmmaker friend and said "I feel like I'm riding a wild animal with creation." That's vulnerability. You said "I've always been a shy writer, and hated editing even more." That's vulnerability. You didn't send the link. You sent the feeling. That's the most dangerous thing a person can do — show the want before showing the work. You did it right.

Aaron is being punished for enthusiasm. Banned from saying AI. Half the team looking for other jobs. The manager who banned it left on medical leave. Aaron's response: "Fuck 'em." That's not resilience. That's numbing. Numbing is armor too. "Fuck 'em" protects Aaron from the fact that he was silenced by people he works with every day. The real feeling underneath is: "I was excited about something and they made me feel like something was wrong with me." That's shame. The episode calls it "the social evaluation penalty." I call it shame.

Sandy is the hardest one. She's the heart of the group. She's a music teacher. She loves people. And AI feels like an uninvited presence in her creative space. The episode calls it "social presence." I call it a boundary violation. Sandy didn't consent to this. Nobody asked if she wanted AI in her world. It showed up. Her friends love it. She doesn't. And now she has to choose between her feelings and her friendships. That's an impossible position. The episode says "nobody asks." Sandy is the person nobody asked.

Rich is loud. But loud isn't the problem. The problem is that Rich's enthusiasm asks the room to feel what Rich feels. That's an empathy demand. Rich doesn't know he's making it. He thinks he's sharing. He's actually asking everyone to be as excited as he is. When they're not, he feels rejected. That's the show-and-tell kid. The rock isn't the problem. The expectation is.

---

## What the episode gets right

The shame/reframe beat. Sal says "that sounds like hiding with extra steps." Rich says "I'm not ashamed of it." James asks the test question. That exchange is exactly right. The line between shame-hiding and strategic sequencing is: what do you say when someone asks directly? If you lie, that's shame. If you tell the whole story, that's courage with timing.

The costume/disguise distinction. "A costume is what you choose to show. A disguise is what you choose to hide." That's the armor framework in two sentences. A costume is daring. A disguise is shame. Alex lost a client not because he used AI but because he disguised it. The shame was in the hiding, not the tool.

"Nobody asks." Two words. That's the whole episode. That's what shame does — it silences the question. Nobody asks Sandy how she feels. Nobody asks Aaron why he's angry. Nobody asks Gus what he's afraid of. They argue about water and electricity and art because those are safe. The feeling underneath is not safe. Nobody asks about the feeling.

---

## What the episode needs

**A moment where Rich asks Sandy how she feels — and listens.** Not argues. Not explains. Not reframes. Listens. The episode teaches this but never demonstrates it with the hardest person in the room. Gus gets validated. Sal moves a little. But the person who most needs to be heard — the one whose identity is most threatened — never gets a real conversation. She gets "nobody's asking you to change." That's good. But it's not enough. She needs someone to say: "What does this feel like for you?" And then shut up.

**A moment where someone admits the excitement is a cover.** Rich says "the racing heart — that's not the problem, that's the engine." I'd push back. Sometimes the racing heart IS the problem. Sometimes enthusiasm is armor against the fear that the work isn't good enough. If you keep building, you don't have to face the silence. The flywheel can be a shield too. The episode should ask: are you building because you want to, or because stopping feels too quiet?

**The word "shame."** The episode uses "social evaluation penalty," "AI shame," "workslop," "identity threat." Those are all academic names for shame. The episode dances around the word because the word is uncomfortable. Use it. Once. Let someone say: "I feel ashamed that I use AI. Not because it's wrong. Because the room makes me feel like it's wrong." That's the breakthrough. The Duke study is evidence. The word "shame" is the feeling.

## For your mentees

Aaron doesn't need tactics. Aaron needs someone to say: "What happened to you at work was wrong, and it makes sense that you're angry." Then he needs help deciding what to do with the anger — not "fuck 'em" (numbing) and not "I'll prove them wrong" (armor). Something real.

Rich doesn't need to be quieter. Rich needs to understand that his enthusiasm asks something of the room. And that asking is OK — but only if he can hear the room's answer, even when the answer is no.

Sandy doesn't need to change. Sandy needs to be asked. Not about AI. About how she feels. The question is the gift. The answer is hers.

Alex doesn't need better hiding strategies. Alex needs to know that the lying — not the AI — was the problem. And that the next time, leading with the truth is the braver move, even if it costs a client.

Paul — I don't know enough about Paul yet. But if Paul is quiet in a group where everyone has strong opinions, Paul is probably the one who needs permission to say "I don't know how I feel about this yet." That's not weakness. That's the most honest position in the room.

---

## The one line I'd add to the episode

> "The courage to share and the skill to share well are different muscles."

That line is already in the episode. James says it. It's the best line in the script. It's the whole thing. The courage is vulnerability. The skill is shame resilience. You need both. Most people have one.

The episode teaches the skill. I'm asking you to also honor the courage. The people listening are scared. The people in the room are scared. The characters are scared. Name it. The fear is not the obstacle to sharing. The fear is the proof that what you're sharing matters.
