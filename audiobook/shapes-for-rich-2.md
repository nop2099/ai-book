[STAGE]
Act One. What a skill actually is.

---

[NARRATOR]
In Episode 1, I told you: when you find yourself explaining the same thing to an AI for the third time, stop explaining and write a skill. That's the one-liner. Here's what it actually means.

[NARRATOR]
A skill is a written procedure. Plain text. Step by step. It has a name, a purpose, a sequence of actions, standards for what good looks like, and a definition of done. Any AI that reads the file can execute the procedure. Any session. Any model. Any time. The skill is model-agnostic. It's not a prompt. A prompt is telling someone what to do once. A skill is teaching them how to do it forever.

[NARRATOR]
You already built one. The bank PDF work. Remember — you pulled your credit card statements, gave the AI naming rules, built a verification spreadsheet, added a changelog. That's a skill. The rules are the steps. The spreadsheet is the standard. The changelog is the definition of done. Everything's there. But it lives in your head and in scattered files. It doesn't live in one document the AI reads automatically.

[NARRATOR]
The move is simple. Write it down. One file. Call it SKILL dot md. Next time you open a session to rename bank statements, the AI reads the file and already knows the naming convention, the verification format, and the changelog requirement. You never explain it again.

[STAGE]
The Starfleet parallel.

---

[NARRATOR]
Here's where Star Trek comes in. Because you've been watching skills your entire life. You just didn't know that's what they were called.

[NARRATOR]
When Picard says "evasive maneuver pattern delta" — he doesn't explain what that means. He doesn't say "hard to port, thirty degrees, drop speed by half, bring the nose up ten degrees." The crew already knows. They trained on it. It's a documented procedure. Any helm officer on any Federation starship can execute pattern delta because the procedure exists in the ship's computer. That's a SKILL dot md file. Same structure. Purpose: evade incoming fire. Steps: the specific sequence of maneuvers. Standards: maintain shield integrity above a threshold. Definition of done: threat has passed or new orders given.

[NARRATOR]
The whole ship runs on these. "Red alert" is a skill — a documented protocol that triggers a specific sequence: shields up, weapons armed, all hands to battle stations, non-essential systems to standby. Nobody explains it each time. The procedure exists. The crew reads it once, trains on it, and executes it perfectly under pressure. That's the whole point. You write the skill so that under pressure, the system works without you re-explaining everything.

[NARRATOR]
Your bank PDF procedure is your first evasive maneuver pattern. You just haven't filed it in the ship's computer yet.

[STAGE]
Act Two. The Incident Command System. Or — how the Enterprise runs a crisis.

---

[NARRATOR]
I sent you an email a couple days ago about something called the Incident Command System. ICS. It was buried in the meeting notes skill writeup. You might have skimmed it. I'm going to re-introduce it now, because I think it'll click differently through Star Trek.

[NARRATOR]
ICS is the framework that real emergency responders use. Firefighters, FEMA, disaster response teams. When something chaotic is happening — a wildfire, a flood, a building collapse — and there's too much information hitting too many people at once, ICS is how they impose structure on that chaos. It's been refined for decades. It works.

[NARRATOR]
And here's the thing: Starfleet already runs on ICS. Not officially. Gene Roddenberry never said "and the Enterprise uses the Incident Command System." But the structures are identical. Because the problem is the same — intelligent people dealing with chaotic real-time information under pressure, needing to make decisions, track resources, and learn from what happened.

[NARRATOR]
Let me show you the mapping.

---

[STAGE]
The SITREP.

---

[NARRATOR]
ICS starts with the SITREP — the Situation Report. Where were we before the incident, where are we now, what changed. Pre-state, post-state. It's the first thing the incident commander needs.

[NARRATOR]
You know what Starfleet calls a SITREP?

[NARRATOR]
The Captain's Log. "Captain's Log, Stardate 47634.8. We have entered the Neutral Zone in response to a distress signal from the science vessel Yosemite." That's a SITREP. Pre-state: we were on patrol. Post-state: we're in the Neutral Zone. What changed: distress signal. Every single Captain's Log entry is a SITREP dressed in Starfleet uniform. It tells the next person who reads the record exactly where things stood.

[NARRATOR]
In the meeting notes skill I sent you, the SITREP section does the same thing for meetings. Where things stood before the call. Where they stand after. Two sentences. Thirty-second read. The most important section in the whole output because it's the one a busy person actually reads.

---

[STAGE]
The Decision Log.

---

[NARRATOR]
ICS tracks every decision. Who made it, when, what information they had, what they chose. Not buried in a narrative. Logged explicitly. Because after the incident, someone needs to reconstruct what happened without reading a hundred pages.

[NARRATOR]
On the Enterprise, this is the Bridge Log. Every command decision recorded. "Mr. Worf, raise shields." That's in the log. "Number One, take us to warp six." Logged. "Doctor, begin evacuating deck twelve." Logged. Not because Picard is a bureaucrat. Because the next shift — the next crew — needs to know what decisions were made and why. If Riker takes the conn at 0200, he doesn't start from zero. He reads the log.

[NARRATOR]
Your bank PDF changelog? That's a decision log. Every rename recorded with the rule that generated it. You built this instinctively. You didn't know it had a name. Now it does.

---

[STAGE]
The After-Action Review.

---

[NARRATOR]
This is the ICS piece most people skip and it's the most important one. After the incident is over, you do an After-Action Review. What did we plan to do? What did we actually do? Why was it different? What should we change?

[NARRATOR]
The AAR is the mission debrief. Every Trek episode with a senior staff meeting after the crisis — "What happened out there? Why did the containment field fail? What would we do differently?" — that's an AAR. It's where the learning happens. Not during the crisis. After. When there's time to think.

[NARRATOR]
In my email, I told you about the post-mortem loop: have the agent review its own work, find the friction, update the skill. That's an AAR for your AI workflow. The meeting notes skill has an explicit section for this in the formal output mode. Most people skip it because they think the meeting is over when the meeting is over. It's not. The meeting is over when you've captured what you learned.

[NARRATOR]
On the Enterprise, the after-action review is why the ship gets better at what it does. Not because the crew changes. Because the procedures update. The next time they face a similar anomaly, the response protocol includes what they learned. The skill improves. The ship compounds.

---

[STAGE]
Resource Tracking.

---

[NARRATOR]
ICS tracks resources obsessively. Firefighters, trucks, water supplies, personnel availability. You can't plan operations without knowing what you have.

[NARRATOR]
On the Enterprise: "Torpedo complement at sixty percent." "Dilithium reserves critical." "Sickbay reports fourteen casualties, three critical." "Engineering needs four hours for hull repairs." That's resource tracking. LaForge doesn't report to the bridge because Picard wants small talk. He reports because the captain needs to know what the ship can actually do before ordering the next maneuver.

[NARRATOR]
For you, resource tracking is knowing what you have to work with. What folders exist. What data is available. What tools you have installed. When you build a ME dot md file — the wall-of-data prompt I sent you — you're building a ship's manifest. You're telling the next away team: here's what this ship carries, here's what the captain cares about, here's where the important cargo is stored. Without that manifest, every session starts from a cold dock. With it, the crew knows the ship before they beam aboard.

---

[STAGE]
Unified Command.

---

[NARRATOR]
ICS has a concept called Unified Command. Multiple agencies — fire, police, medical, hazmat — each with different expertise, different priorities, different chains of command. But they serve the same incident. They share the same objective. They coordinate, not compete.

[NARRATOR]
That's the bridge crew. Worf runs tactical. LaForge runs engineering. Crusher runs medical. Troi handles the human factor. Data handles operations. They don't agree on everything. Worf wants to raise shields; Troi says the aliens are scared, not hostile. But they serve the same ship. Picard hears both. Picard decides.

[NARRATOR]
That's what it looks like when you have multiple skills working together. Your bank PDF skill and a future recipe management skill don't need to share architecture. They don't need to agree on file formats. They serve the same captain — you. Same bridge, different stations. The skills are your department heads. Each one expert in its domain. You're the one who hears them all and decides.

[STAGE]
Act Three. The anatomy of a real skill.

---

[NARRATOR]
Let me walk through the meeting notes skill I sent you — the one with the SKILL dot md file and the pipeline. I want you to see it through the Starfleet lens, because every piece maps.

[NARRATOR]
The SKILL dot md is the procedure manual. It tells any AI — any crew member — how to process a meeting recording into structured notes. Step by step. It doesn't assume the crew member has done this before. It doesn't assume they were on the last mission. It assumes nothing except that they can read the file and follow the instructions.

[NARRATOR]
Step one: inspect the recording for embedded subtitle streams. That's the sensor sweep. Before the away team does anything, they scan the environment. What's there? What format is it in? What can we extract without touching it?

[NARRATOR]
Steps two through five: extract captions, pull audio, run speech-to-text, build a hybrid transcript. That's the data collection phase. The away team gathering samples, running their tricorders, building the evidence base.

[NARRATOR]
Step nine: the redaction pass. This was yours, Rich. You asked "what if someone doesn't want their employer mentioned?" and that instinct became a real feature. Context-sensitive substitutions — "Amazon" becomes "the company" or "my employer." Not bracket-REDACTED. Natural phrasing, as if the name never came up. Your privacy instinct wrote a procedure that protects people. That's exactly how procedures should get written. Someone notices a gap. The gap becomes a step. The step lives in the file forever.

[NARRATOR]
The output structure has five layers. Layer one is the SITREP — what happened, thirty-second read. Layer two is action items — who does what. Layer three is detailed analysis — meeting arc, decisions, friction points, achievements. Layer four is for the person running the meeting — a confidence check, energy map, patterns. Layer five is reference — full timeline, transcript, the raw material.

[NARRATOR]
Five layers. Each one is a different level of the ship's computer. The captain reads layer one. The department heads read layer two. The science officer reads layer three. The ship's counselor reads layer four. And layer five is the sensor logs — everything, for the record, so anyone can go back to the raw data.

[STAGE]
The flywheel.

---

[NARRATOR]
Here's the part that makes a skill different from a script you run once.

[NARRATOR]
Every time the meeting notes skill processes a recording, it gets a little better. Difficulties get logged. Pipeline issues update the process manual. If the speech-to-text stumbled on a name, the next run knows to watch for it. If a section was consistently empty or unhelpful, it gets revised or removed. The skill is alive in the way that Starfleet procedures are alive — after every mission, the procedure gets reviewed. The ones that work stay. The ones that don't get updated. The ship's library is not a museum. It's an operating system.

[NARRATOR]
That's the post-mortem loop I mentioned in my email. Have the agent review its own output. What worked. What was friction. What should change. Then update the SKILL dot md. The next run inherits the improvement. Skills compound. Better procedures, better output, less friction, more trust. The first run is always the roughest. By the fifth run, the crew can execute it in their sleep.

The return.

I want to bring it back to one thing.

[NARRATOR]
The reason Starfleet works — the reason the Enterprise survives week after week with the same crew and keeps getting better — is not that the crew is brilliant. They are. But the reason the ship improves is that the procedures improve. The knowledge lives in the ship's computer, not just in the officers' heads. When a crew member transfers, the next one reads the same procedures and picks up where they left off. The durable advantage is the documentation. The institutional knowledge. The written-down version of what works.

[NARRATOR]
That's what you're building when you write a skill. Not software. Not a script. Institutional knowledge. The kind that traditionally lives in experienced heads and walks out the door when the person leaves. Except now it lives in files. And it compounds. And it works at three in the morning while you sleep.

[NARRATOR]
You asked about skills. That's what they are. Starfleet procedures for your life. Written down, filed in the ship's computer, executable by any crew member without the captain explaining it every time.
