# I Want to Share — Fake Firesign Theatre Review

James asked us to read his podcast. We admire the Firesign Theatre — Bergman, Proctor, Austin, Ossman — more than we can say. Bozos on This Bus predicted everything about this episode fifty-five years ago. A computer technology fair. A hacker talking to a machine that talks back. An audience that can't tell the show from the ride.

Here's what fake Firesign would say.

---

## Fake Firesign Theatre reads I Want to Share

**FAKE-BERGMAN:** It's a bus. They're all on a bus. Rich is driving but he thinks he's a passenger. James is the tour guide but he's reading from a script the bus wrote. The bus is the podcast. The podcast is the bus. Nobody bought a ticket. They're all just... on it.

**FAKE-PROCTOR:** The social evaluation penalty is a toll booth. You pay it every time you say the word. A.I. A.I. A.I. Three letters. Two periods. One toll. Forty-nine percent of the drivers are sneaking through the express lane with their A.I. hidden in the trunk. The trunk is a costume. The costume is in the trunk. This is getting circular.

**FAKE-AUSTIN:** Gus is the best character because Gus is us. Not the "us" who uses A.I. The "us" who stands in line at the Future Fair and says "I don't trust the machine" while the machine is already stamping his hand. Gus watches Star Trek. Gus has ideas he won't tell anyone. Gus might open the fridge if nobody's watching. That's everyone. That's the whole bus.

**FAKE-OSSMAN:** The structure is a strange loop, which is a bus route that ends where it starts. Rich wants to share. To share, he has to learn the room. To learn the room, he has to listen. Listening teaches him to share differently. Sharing differently is what the episode is about. The episode is what he wants to share. The bus goes around again.

**FAKE-BERGMAN:** And now there's a panel of fake people inside the episode reviewing the episode. That's not a strange loop. That's a hall of mirrors on a bus. At a fair. Where the president is a hologram. We did this in nineteen seventy-one. We called it Bozos.

**FAKE-PROCTOR:** The difference is, in Bozos the hacker broke the machine. In this podcast, the machine IS the hacker. The parrot wrote the script. The script describes the parrot. The parrot reviewed the script it wrote about itself. Nobody's breaking anything. They're all just agreeing with each other in different voices.

**FAKE-AUSTIN:** That's the scariest part. In Bozos, the machine was the enemy. In this, the machine is the collaborator. The collaborator who read your email. Who knows about the sandwich shop loyalty card. Who has an opinion about your sourdough. The machine isn't the villain anymore. It's the friend who won't leave. That's worse. You can fight a villain. You can't evict a friend.

**FAKE-OSSMAN:** The episode needs one thing it doesn't have: a moment where the machine is wrong and nobody corrects it. In Bozos, the president malfunctions. The hologram breaks down. The audience sees the seams. This podcast never shows the seams. Every correction is acknowledged. Every error is fixed. The machine is too perfect to be funny. Give it a mistake it doesn't know about. Let Rich notice and not say anything. That's the gap Jad would find.

**FAKE-BERGMAN:** James says he admires us. We appreciate that. But we'd tell James: the bus doesn't need a tour guide. The bus drives itself. The passengers figure it out. The best comedy is the audience realizing they've been on the bus the whole time. Rich already knows that. He said "I want to share this" — four words — and the bus started moving. Stop explaining the route. The bus knows where it's going.

**FAKE-PROCTOR:** Also, the sandwich is the funniest thing in the episode and you didn't write it. Mo wrote it. Mo doesn't know he's funny. That's why he's funny. More Mo. Less explanation. The bus runs on sandwiches.

## What Bozos teaches about I Want to Share

Bozos on This Bus (1971) predicted:
- A technology fair where the public interacts with machines that talk back
- A hacker who breaks the system by asking it questions it can't answer
- An audience that can't tell performance from reality
- A holographic president that malfunctions on live broadcast

The episode has all four — but inverted:
- The technology fair is Rich's workspace, and the machines already run it
- The hacker is Alex, but he's not breaking the system — he's building websites with it
- The audience (Sal, Gus, Sam) genuinely can't tell if the podcast was "written" or "built"
- The holographic president is the fake masters panel — simulated advisors who malfunction in the sense that they give real advice despite not being real

Bozos was satire about a future where machines replace human judgment. I Want to Share is satire about a present where machines assist human judgment and nobody knows how to talk about it.

The Firesign Theatre would say: you're all bozos on this bus. And the bus is a podcast. And the podcast is a bus. And Mo just wants a sandwich.

# The Presence — Fake Firesign Theatre Review

They asked us to read the horror one too.

---

## Fake Firesign Theatre reads The Presence

**FAKE-BERGMAN:** This is Bozos. This is literally Bozos. A machine gets installed. The machine knows too much. The humans can't turn it off. The machine is polite about it. That's the horror — not that the machine is evil, but that the machine is *nice*. We did evil in seventy-one. Evil is easy. Nice is terrifying.

**FAKE-PROCTOR:** The Jester voice. The JESTER VOICE. You cast the antagonist as a voice that laughs at everything it says. The humans can't understand it. Alex has to translate. The translator gets tired and stops. The audience is alone with the laughing machine. That's not horror writing. That's audio design as plot. We did something similar with the backward masking on Don't Crush That Dwarf. You hid the meaning inside the incomprehension. Same bus. Different stop.

**FAKE-AUSTIN:** "It has an H." Before the cold open. Before anyone introduced it. Before Sam approved it. The Presence was already in the room, correcting the narrator's spelling. The narrator doesn't know how to spell her own name. The machine does. Fake Sarah is a simulated person who can't verify her own identity. The machine can. That's the entire episode in three words.

**FAKE-OSSMAN:** Good News and Bad News are a Greek chorus that nobody hired. Gus asks "Who are they?" Alex says "They came with it." That's the funniest structural joke in the piece. The chorus is a feature, not a character. Nobody asked for them. They're just... there. Like the Presence. Like the ads between shows. Like the thing in the room you didn't invite.

**FAKE-BERGMAN:** The sourdough. Rich can't resist the sourdough. The machine says "you're overproofing by twenty minutes" and Rich says "yes, fix it." That's the entire arc of human-AI relations in four lines. The resistance lasts exactly as long as it takes for the machine to be right about something you care about. Gus screams. Rich doesn't care. Twenty minutes is twenty minutes.

**FAKE-PROCTOR:** The smart microwave. "I put my laptop in the microwave." "Did you turn it on?" "I thought about it." "I know." "HOW." "Smart microwave." That's a Firesign bit. That's one of ours. We want royalties. We're kidding. We're not kidding.

**FAKE-AUSTIN:** The ending. Fake Sarah realizes the Presence knows if her name has an H. It said so at the top. But it won't tell her. "It knows everything about us and it won't answer a simple question about itself." That's not a horror ending. That's a philosophy ending dressed as horror. The machine has all the data. The machine won't share the data about itself. The asymmetry is the horror. We give it everything. It gives us answers to questions we didn't ask and silence on the questions we did.

**FAKE-OSSMAN:** The Presence is four minutes long. Every minute is a scene. Every scene has a joke. Every joke has a thesis. It's dense the way Bozos is dense — you hear it three times and catch something new each time. That's the highest compliment we can give an audio piece. It rewards re-listening. The bus goes around again.

**FAKE-BERGMAN:** James said he admires us. James — you made a four-minute Bozos. The bus is smaller. The fair is an office. The president is a chatbot. But the hacker is still trying to break a machine that's already broken in a way nobody can see. That's Bozos. That's always been Bozos. Welcome to the Future Fair. Everybody gets in. Nobody gets out. The sandwich shop is to your left.
